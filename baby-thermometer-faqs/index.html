Baby thermometer questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The most accurate baby thermometer for at-home use is a rectal thermometer, but temporal artery and ear thermometers offer fast convenience once your baby is a few months old. Choose the method that fits your baby’s age and your comfort level, and always follow the manufacturer’s cleaning instructions.

Which type of baby thermometer is most accurate?

The gold standard is a rectal thermometer, which measures core body temperature with the least variability. For newborns and infants under three months, pediatricians usually recommend a rectal reading as the most reliable indicator of fever. Temporal artery (forehead) and ear (tympanic) thermometers are very popular for their speed and ease of use once your baby is a bit older. They are generally accurate when used correctly, but earwax or an improper angle can skew ear readings, and forehead models must be held at the right distance. Ear thermometers are typically suitable after six months, while temporal artery units often work from birth.

How do I take my baby’s temperature safely?

Always start by cleaning the thermometer according to the package directions. For a rectal reading, use a dedicated thermometer labeled for rectal use, apply a small amount of petroleum jelly to the tip, then gently insert it about half an inch to an inch (just past the tip). Hold your baby still on their back with their legs tucked, and remove it once it beeps. For ear or forehead readings, position the thermometer as the manual instructs. Avoid using an ear thermometer on a baby with noticeable ear drainage or after a bath, when the ear canal might be moist. Never force a thermometer into any orifice, and never leave a baby unsupervised with one.

What temperature is a fever in babies?

A fever is generally defined as a core body temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher when measured rectally. For armpit (axillary) readings, the threshold is about 99°F (37.2°C) or above, and for ear or forehead readings, the normal cut-off often sits around 100°F (37.8°C) depending on the device. Keep in mind that a baby’s temperature can fluctuate slightly with activity, time of day, and heavy clothing. The most important step is to use the same method consistently so you can track changes. If your baby is under three months old and has a reported fever, call your pediatrician right away.

How often should I replace the batteries or probe covers?

Most digital baby thermometers run on small watch-style batteries that last for many months to a year with regular use. You’ll know it’s time to replace them when the display dims or the unit fails to turn on. Keep a spare battery or two on hand, especially if you travel often. Probe covers for ear and rectal thermometers are disposable and should be replaced after each use to prevent cross-contamination. If you use a brand that relies on covers, buy a bulk pack so you never run out at a critical moment. For temporal artery thermometers, there are usually no covers to replace; just wipe the lens with a soft cloth between uses.

Can I use a forehead thermometer for newborns?

Many temporal artery thermometers are cleared for use from birth, but their accuracy in newborns is less established compared to rectal readings. Several pediatric studies have found that forehead scans can miss low-grade fevers in infants under three months, so most experts still recommend a rectal thermometer for the first few months. If you decide to use a forehead thermometer with a newborn, follow the manufacturer’s age-specific instructions closely and be prepared to confirm any rise in temperature with a rectal reading before calling your doctor.

What features should I look for in a baby thermometer?

Look for a model that gives readings in under three seconds if you want to minimize fussing. An illuminated display and flexible tip are helpful for night-time checks and comfort. Many modern thermometers also offer color-coded fever alerts (green for normal, yellow for elevated, red for fever) and memory recall to track trends. If you prefer an ear thermometer, make sure it has a pre-warmed tip to avoid a falsely low reading from the cold sensor. For temporal artery units, check that it’s marked for babies and that the lens is easy to clean. Avoid any product with complicated steps that might slow you down when your baby is upset.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best thermometer for a newborn?

For the first three months, a rectal thermometer is considered the most reliable. Brands like FridaBaby and Safety 1st offer easy-to-read digital rectal thermometers with flexible tips designed for infant use.

Can I use a pacifier thermometer?

Pacifier thermometers are convenient for older babies who accept a pacifier, but they are less accurate than rectal or ear models because the reading depends on the baby keeping the pacifier still in their mouth for the required amount of time. They are best used as a quick check rather than a definitive fever diagnosis.

How do I clean a rectal thermometer?

After each use, wash the tip with warm soapy water or wipe it with an alcohol swab. Avoid submerging the entire unit unless it is labeled waterproof. Let it air dry completely before storing it in its case, and never share a rectal thermometer with another child without thorough cleaning.

Do ear thermometers require a specific technique?

Yes. Gently pull the baby’s outer ear straight back to straighten the ear canal, then insert the probe snugly but not forcefully. The reading can be off if the probe is not fully sealed. Some ear thermometers also have a pre-warmed tip; let it sit in the ear for a moment after insertion before pressing the button.

What about no-touch forehead thermometers?

No-touch (infrared) thermometers are very popular for their hygiene and ease during sleep. They are generally reliable for screening in older babies and children, but they tend to be less precise than in-ear or rectal models, especially if the room is drafty or the baby’s forehead is sweaty. Use them as a quick check and confirm with another method if you see a high reading.

How do I know if my baby has a fever without a thermometer?

You may notice your baby feels warm to the touch, is unusually fussy or lethargic, has flushed cheeks, or is sweating. However, these signs are not reliable on their own. A digital thermometer is the only way to confirm a fever accurately, so always keep one in your home medicine kit.

In shortChoosing the right baby thermometer comes down to your baby’s age, your comfort with the method, and how quickly you need a reading. Whatever type you pick, use it consistently, clean it after every use, and trust your instincts, if your baby seems unwell, a measured temperature is just one part of the picture. When in doubt, your pediatrician is always the best guide.
