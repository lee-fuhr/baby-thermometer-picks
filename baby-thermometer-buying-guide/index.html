How to choose a baby thermometer

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For the most accurate readings in infants under three months, stick to digital rectal thermometers, they are the gold standard. For quick, non-invasive checks on older babies and toddlers, a well-rated temporal artery or ear thermometer offers convenience without sacrificing much reliability.

Which type of baby thermometer is most accurate?

When you need to know your baby’s true temperature, especially for a newborn under three months, a digital rectal thermometer is the only choice recommended by pediatricians. The rectal route gives a core temperature reading that closely matches the body’s internal heat. For older babies and toddlers, temporal artery (forehead) and ear (tympanic) thermometers are very reliable, but only if used exactly as directed. Temporal thermometers scan the skin over the temporal artery; ear thermometers need a clean, straight ear canal. Both are far faster than rectal and less upsetting for the child. Armpit (axillary) thermometers are the least accurate and should be used only for rough screening. A reading 1 to 2 degrees lower than rectal is normal, so you must add a degree when interpreting. Pacifier thermometers are convenient but often drift in accuracy if the baby’s mouth isn’t tightly sealed.

What features make a baby thermometer worth buying?

Speed matters, a crying baby won’t hold still. Look for a thermometer that gives a reading in under 10 seconds. Rectal digital thermometers typically take 30 to 60 seconds, but many ear and forehead models deliver results in one to three seconds. A backlit display is a small luxury that makes a big difference during middle-of-the-night checks. Fever alert systems that light up or beep when the temperature crosses a preset threshold (usually 100.4°F / 38°C) spare you from squinting at tiny numbers. Memory recall for the last several readings lets you track a fever’s progression without writing everything down. Also consider whether the thermometer is waterproof for easy cleaning, rectal and axillary probes need thorough disinfection after each use.

How do I choose a thermometer for a newborn versus a toddler?

For newborns (birth to three months), the choice is simple: a plain digital rectal thermometer. Skip fancy forehead or ear models until the baby is at least three to six months old, because their ear canals are too narrow and their forehead skin too thin for reliable non-invasive readings. Once your baby hits six months and beyond, a temporal artery or ear thermometer becomes a practical everyday tool. Many parents keep one of each: a rectal stick for when they truly need a precise number, and a temporal scanner for quick checks when the child just feels warm. Toddlers who resist any insertion often tolerate a no-contact forehead thermometer best, though accuracy can vary if the room is drafty or the child is sweating.

What common mistakes do parents make when taking a baby’s temperature?

The biggest error is using the wrong method for the baby’s age. Relying on an armpit or pacifier reading for a newborn can miss a dangerous fever. Also, taking a temperature right after a bath, after the baby has been bundled in a blanket, or immediately after feeding can produce falsely high readings. Positioning matters: for rectal thermometers, the tip should be gently inserted only about half an inch to an inch. For ear thermometers, the probe must point toward the opposite eye, not straight in, to catch the eardrum. Failing to clean the thermometer between uses is another common slip. A quick wipe with alcohol is not enough; follow the manufacturer’s instructions, which usually involve washing with soap and cool water (not boiling) or using a designated disinfectant wipe.

Is a no-touch forehead thermometer good enough for daily use?

No-touch or infrared forehead thermometers are incredibly convenient, no contact, no waking a sleeping baby, and no arguing with a toddler. Many pediatricians’ offices use them for quick screenings. However, they are the least accurate of the non-invasive options. Environmental factors like room temperature, sweat, or a fan blowing directly on the baby can throw off the reading by a degree or more. They are excellent for monitoring trends over time (e.g., “is the fever climbing?”) but not for a one-time definitive diagnosis. If you opt for a no-touch model, buy one from a well-known medical brand and test it against a rectal thermometer a few times so you understand the typical difference. Consider it a first-pass screening tool, not a replacement for an accurate contact method when you really need to know.

How much should I expect to spend on a good baby thermometer?

You can find reliable digital rectal thermometers for a very affordable price, typically under the cost of a few coffees. They are simple, durable, and often come as a multi-pack for different family members. Mid-priced temporal and ear thermometers usually cost a bit more than basic rectal models, often falling in the range you’d spend on a nice dinner out. Premium no-touch or smart thermometers that sync with an app cost a step up from that, but the extra investment buys convenience and sometimes a fever-tracking history. No matter your budget, stick with established brands that have FDA clearance (or the equivalent in your country) and a good reputation for accuracy. A cheap no-name thermometer can save money upfront but cost you peace of mind later.

Frequently asked questions

Is it safe to use a rectal thermometer on a newborn?

Yes, it’s perfectly safe when done gently. Use a thermometer specifically labeled for rectal use, apply a small amount of lubricant (plain petroleum jelly works), and insert only about half an inch to an inch. Always hold the thermometer in place and never force it.

What is a normal temperature for a baby?

A normal rectal temperature is around 98.6°F (37°C), but it can range from 97.9°F to 100.4°F (36.6°C to 38°C). Oral and ear temperatures run about 0.5°F to 1°F lower. A fever is generally considered a rectal reading of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher.

Can I use an ear thermometer on a baby under six months?

Most ear thermometer manufacturers recommend waiting until a baby is at least six months old because the ear canal is too narrow and curved for an accurate reading before then. For younger infants, stick with rectal or temporal artery methods.

How do I clean a baby thermometer without damaging it?

For digital rectal thermometers, wash the probe with cool water and mild soap, then wipe with isopropyl alcohol. Do not submerge the entire unit unless it is waterproof. Ear and forehead thermometers should be cleaned with a soft, damp cloth or an alcohol wipe, never immerse them.

Do smart thermometers that connect to my phone really help?

They can be useful for parents who want to track fever trends over time or share readings with a pediatrician remotely. The main downside is battery dependency and the occasional pairing hiccup. The temperature reading itself is not inherently more accurate than a basic model from the same brand.

Why does my baby’s temperature vary when I take it in different spots?

Normal body temperature is not uniform, the rectum gives the closest core reading, while the forehead, ear, and armpit are surface measurements that can vary by 0.5°F to 2°F. That’s why consistency matters: always use the same method and spot to track changes over time.

In shortThe best baby thermometer is the one you use correctly and consistently. Start with a reliable digital rectal model for the first few months, then add a fast forehead or ear thermometer for everyday checks as your child grows. Keep a spare set of batteries, read the instructions once, and trust your instincts, you know when your baby feels warm, even if the numbers take a moment to confirm it.
